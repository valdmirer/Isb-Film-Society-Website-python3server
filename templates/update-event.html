Screening - Past Lives
About and Questions

A Great Pick By Nargis & Moneeb 

Past Lives is a masterstroke of programming from Nargis and Moneeb for the July 13th screening-For both moviegoers and the international community in Islamabad, its touching reflection on cultural displacement and "what if" nostalgia will strike a deep chord. A summer evening under the stars is the ideal setting for the film's subdued force (those lingering silences! the unsolved tension!) so that everyone may sit with its emotional weight. Anticipate deep discussions on fate and identity to organically arise as the credits roll at dusk-exactly the kind of cinematic discourse Pakistan Film Society fosters. A daring decision that demonstrates their curatorial vision's ability to promote genuine artistic contemplation beyond mere amusement.

Theme Alignment : Mirrors Pakistan Film Society's mission to explore cross-cultural stories
Discussion Potential : The Korean in-yun concept parallels South Asian ideas of kismet
Visual Poetry : Celine Song's minimalist frames will shine in your outdoor setup

Past Lives (2023): A Deep Emotional Journey 

Past Lives is a quietly powerful film that lingers in the hearts and minds of its audience long after the credits roll. Directed by Celine Song, this semi-autobiographical story explores love, identity, and the roads not taken through the lens of a decades-spanning connection between two childhood friends.

Past Lives follows Nora and Hae Sung, childhood friends in Seoul separated when Nora's family emigrates to Canada. Twelve years later, they reconnect online, sparking a fleeting long-distance bond that fades as life pulls them apart-Nora to become a writer in New York, Hae Sung to an ordinary life in Seoul. When they reunite in person decades later during Hae Sung's visit to New York, their brief, tender encounters-filled with unspoken longing and cultural dissonance-force them to confront the life they might have shared, all while Nora's American husband watches with quiet understanding, culminating in a heartbreakingly silent farewell that lingers like a haunting question about fate, choice, and the invisible threads connecting us across time and distance.

Awakens Nostalgia & Personal Reflection 

The movie appeals to everyone's sense of nostalgia, not only for past lovers but also for past self. Many times, viewers find themselves thinking back on their own "what if" scenarios, early relationships, or cultural heritage. It becomes impossible to resist the question, "What if I had chosen a different path?" 

The Korean concept of in-yun -the idea that relationships are tied across multiple lifetimes-runs through the film. It leaves audiences debating:
Are some connections destined?
Do we romanticize the past because it's unresolved?
How much of our identity is shaped by the lives we didn't live?
